UB Winter Weather Policies and Tips:

How Class Cancellations Work:
The Office of Emergency Management monitors weather conditions and consults with the Winter Weather Advisory Group, which meets as early as 5:00 AM to discuss whether conditions warrant recommending to the university president that classes be canceled or delayed. Both in-person and remote classes are affected by weather cancellations.

Getting Notified About Cancellations:
Register for UB Alert to receive emergency text messages and emails about class cancellations and campus emergencies. You can also check the 645-NEWS hotline (716-645-6397), UB's social media accounts, and local news outlets. Make sure your UB Alert contact information is up to date — many students miss notifications because they haven't updated their phone number.

Student Flexibility During Bad Weather:
Students who cannot safely reach campus due to weather conditions will not be penalized. Faculty are expected to provide reasonable accommodations for students who miss class, an assignment, or an exam because of hazardous travel conditions. However, students must make up missed work promptly once conditions improve. If classes aren't officially canceled but you feel unsafe driving, use your best judgment and email your professor.

Driving Safety:
If your car gets stuck or won't start, contact Motorist Assistance at (716) 645-3943 for jumpstarts or de-icing help (available 8:30 AM to 4:30 PM weekdays). Keep an emergency kit in your car: ice scraper, small shovel, jumper cables, blanket, flashlight, and phone charger.

Snow Removal:
Report snow removal needs on campus to customer service at (716) 645-2025. Sidewalks and main walkways are cleared first, followed by parking lots. Be patient during heavy snowfalls — the grounds crew works around the clock but Buffalo can get feet of snow overnight.

Personal Safety in Winter:
Avoid overexertion in cold weather. The strain from walking through heavy snow drifts or shoveling can be dangerous. Be alert for frostbite, which can happen quickly in sub-freezing temperatures, particularly affecting ears, nose, cheeks, and fingers. Watch for black ice on walkways and parking lots — it's nearly invisible and extremely slippery.
